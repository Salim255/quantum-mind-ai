Randomness in Quantum Mechanics

Quantum mechanics introduces genuine randomness in the outcomes of measurements.

For example, if we measure the spin of electrons first in one direction and then in another, the results form a sequence of outcomes like Ns and Ss that appear completely random. Each new measurement does not allow us to predict the next result with certainty.

This behavior is similar to classical randomness examples like coin tosses, where we also get unpredictable sequences of heads and tails.

Classical randomness (coin toss)

In classical physics, a coin toss is not truly random. If we knew all the exact initial conditions (force, angle, position, air resistance, etc.), we could in principle predict the result exactly.

The randomness we observe comes from small variations we cannot control or measure precisely — not from the system itself.

So classical randomness is apparent randomness, caused by sensitivity to initial conditions.

Quantum randomness

In quantum mechanics, randomness is fundamentally different.

Even with complete knowledge of the system, the outcome of a measurement cannot be predicted exactly. The result is inherently probabilistic.

This means the sequence of measurement outcomes is considered truly random, not just unpredictable due to missing information.

Interpretation question

This idea led to deep debate in physics. Einstein believed that quantum randomness might hide deeper unknown variables that would make outcomes deterministic.

This is known as the hidden variable hypothesis.

What we know today

Experiments designed to test hidden variable theories have been performed many times. The results strongly support the idea that quantum randomness is real and cannot be explained by simple hidden hidden variables

Why it matters ?

This randomness is a core feature of quantum systems like electron spin and photon polarization, which are used to define the concept of a qubit.
