Spin and the Stern–Gerlach Experiment

Classical computers store information using bits, which can be either 0 or 1. Quantum computers use qubits, which can represent 0, 1, or a quantum combination of both states.

One way to create a qubit is by using the spin of an electron. Spin behaves like a tiny magnet with a north and south pole.

In the 1920s, physicists Otto Stern and Walther Gerlach designed an experiment to study this behavior. They sent a beam of silver atoms through a specially shaped magnetic field.

Classically, scientists expected the atoms to be deflected by different amounts, producing a continuous band on a screen. Instead, they observed only two distinct spots.

This surprising result showed that the magnetic orientation of the atoms was not continuous. When measured along a given direction, the atoms could only be found in one of two possible states.

The same behavior is observed for electrons. If we measure an electron's spin in the vertical direction, the result is always one of two outcomes:

  • Spin Up (↑)
  • Spin Down (↓)

These two states form the basis of a qubit.

The Stern–Gerlach experiment was one of the first demonstrations that nature behaves differently from classical expectations. Instead of allowing every possible orientation, quantum measurements produce specific discrete outcomes.

This idea lies at the heart of quantum computing: information can be encoded in quantum states and manipulated in ways that have no classical equivalent.
